Is this proposal's theory of change supported by evidence? It assumes community dialogue alone will reduce intercommunal violence in Darfur.
Peace Intel

The proposal's core assumption — that dialogue alone reduces intercommunal violence — has mixed support. The strongest evidence concerns peacekeeping presence: deployments of several thousand troops and several hundred police dramatically reduced civilian killings across African civil wars, 1991–2008. [synthesis]

On Darfur specifically, intercommunal dialogue during UNAMID repeatedly stalled without protection guarantees; localized agreements in 2009–2010 broke down within months of force drawdowns. [case fact]

A field experiment on grassroots reconciliation points the same way — dialogue helped only alongside other measures — though only the abstract is on platform; the full text sits behind a journal paywall.

One lower-confidence signal: a 2024 situation brief found on the open web, outside the vetted library, reports renewed local ceasefire talks in West Darfur. Treat it as unverified until corroborated.

Bottom line: the well-evidenced finding is that dialogue works as a component of a protection strategy, not on its own. You may want to ask the applicant how security guarantees are handled.
